📊 Kinematics & Motion

1. The Velocity Trap: You are driving around a circular track at a constant 30 mph. Is your velocity constant? Why or why not?

Check Reasoning
No. Velocity is a vector (speed + direction). Because you are turning, your direction is constantly changing, so your velocity is constantly changing, even though your speed is constant.

2. Free Fall Acceleration: A heavy lead ball and a light tennis ball are dropped in a vacuum. Which one has greater acceleration?

Check Reasoning
Neither. In a vacuum, all objects fall with the same constant acceleration (g ≈ 9.8 m/s²) regardless of their mass.

3. Projectile Peak: At the very top of a projectile's path (like a ball thrown upward), what is the instantaneous velocity and the acceleration?

Check Reasoning
Velocity = 0 m/s (vertically); Acceleration = 9.8 m/s² downward. Note: Acceleration is NOT zero at the top! Gravity never stops pulling.

⚖️ Forces & Equilibrium

4. Dynamic Equilibrium: You push a piano across the floor at a constant speed in a straight line. Is the piano in equilibrium? What is the net force?

Check Reasoning
Yes, it is in Dynamic Equilibrium. Since speed is constant and direction is straight, acceleration is zero. Therefore, Net Force = 0 (your push exactly equals the friction).

5. Friction & Mass: A truck locks its brakes and skids to a stop. If the truck's mass were doubled, how would the skidding distance change?

Check Reasoning
The skidding distance stays the same. While a heavier truck has more momentum, it also has more normal force, which creates more friction. The mass cancels out in the energy-work equation.

🔋 Work, Energy & Power

6. The Triple Speed Rule: A car's speed increases from 10 m/s to 30 m/s (tripled). By what factor does its Kinetic Energy increase?

Check Reasoning
9 times. KE = ½mv². Since velocity is squared, 3² = 9.

7. Work vs. Distance: Nellie Newton pushes an object with 2x the force for 2x the distance. How much work did she do compared to before?

Check Reasoning
4 times the work. W = F * d. (2F) * (2d) = 4Fd.

🎾 Momentum & Impulse

8. The Bug vs. Bus: An unfortunate bug splatters against the windshield of a moving bus. Compare the force on the bug to the force on the bus.

Check Reasoning
The forces are exactly equal (Newton's 3rd Law). However, the bug has much less mass, so it undergoes a much larger (and fatal) deceleration.

9. Impact Time: Why should you bend your knees when jumping off a step? Explain using Impulse.

Check Reasoning
Bending your knees increases the time of the impact. Because the change in momentum (Δp) is the same either way, a longer time (Δt) results in a smaller average force on your body.

10. Recoil Science: A rocket accelerates in the vacuum of space. What does the rocket push against to move forward?

Check Reasoning
It pushes against its own exhaust gases. Newton's 3rd Law: The rocket pushes the gas out the back; the gas pushes the rocket forward. It does NOT need to push against air or the ground.

📜 Newton's Laws Final Check

11. Acceleration Ratios: Shopping cart A has 5x the mass of cart B. If they are pushed with the same force, what is the acceleration of cart A compared to B?

Check Reasoning
Cart A will have 1/5th the acceleration of Cart B ($a = F/m$).

12. Scaled Weight: You stand on a scale in an elevator that is accelerating upward. Does the scale show more, less, or your normal weight?

Check Reasoning
More. The floor has to push up harder to accelerate you upward, which compresses the springs in the scale more.
